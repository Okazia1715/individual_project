# Domain Research

The development and implementation of nanomaterials across various
industrial fields, including biotechnology, pharmacology, medical and
food technologies, necessitate investigation and evaluation of their
potential biological activity. In particular, an important research
direction is the assessment of the effects of nanoparticles such as
iron, titanium, and silicon oxides, which are widely used in pigments,
bioimplants, and food products [1]. For example, over the past decade,
the use of titanium dioxide (TiO₂) as a colouring agent in a wide range
of commercial products has significantly increased, and on a global
scale, it now accounts for approximately 70% of the total volume of
composite materials (about 10 million tons per year). An important
feature of industrial TiO₂ production is that nearly one-third of this
amount consists of nanoscale particles [2].

Although many of these compounds are generally considered biologically
inert, numerous studies indicate that, in nanoscale form (particle
diameter < 100 nm, particularly 5–50 nm), they can penetrate cell
membranes, including those of the respiratory tract and gastrointestinal
tract. This can lead to alterations in intracellular signalling
pathways, metabolic dysfunction, genotoxic effects, and even apoptotic
cell death [3].

The gastrointestinal tract represents the main and most common route of
nanoparticle entry into the organism. In particular, the stomach and
intestines are the first gastrointestinal organs in which nanoparticles
can remain for prolonged periods, penetrate into smooth muscle layers
and adipocytes, accumulate, and induce cytotoxic effects [4].
Therefore, investigating the functional and structural characteristics
of gastrointestinal tissues under conditions of chronic exposure to
nanoparticles is of high scientific relevance.

Here, modern digital and computational tools become especially important
for analyzing biological images. In this project, machine learning
methods are used to study microscopic images of healthy gastrointestinal
tissue. The Cellpose model is trained to recognize different cell types,
including adipocytes and smooth muscle cells, and to identify
nanoparticles inside these cells. This automated approach allows for
consistent analysis of cell morphology and provides a quantitative way
to assess nanoparticle localization and accumulation within biological
tissues.
