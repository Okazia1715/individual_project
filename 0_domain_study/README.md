# README

Throughout my research with microscopy images of gastrointestinal
tissues, I have recognized an important methodological limitation:
the lack of a reproducible and scalable method to measure the
localization of nanoparticles inside cells. In experiments on
nanoparticle exposure, it is common for investigators to use manual
segmentation of cells, followed by visual inspection to determine
the localization of nanoparticles inside or outside the boundaries
of cells. This approach may be acceptable for a limited number of
experiments, but it is soon recognized as inadequate when the scale
of experiments is increased.

This manual approach is, by its very nature, time-consuming and
requires considerable human effort, especially when working with
high-resolution microscopy images. More importantly, it also injects
an element of subjectivity into the research workflow, where the
determination of the boundaries of cells and the localization of
nanoparticles may vary between different investigators. This is
especially true even within the same research group, where different
investigators may have different interpretations, resulting in
inconsistencies in results. This inter-observer variability is an
important limitation, especially when the number of experiments is
increased to hundreds or even thousands of images.

In fact, nanoparticle exposure studies in modern times commonly
result in the production of large volumes of imaging data that are
complex in nature. However, the analytical process that follows the
experiment has not always kept pace with the developments made in
experimentation, creating a gap between biological experimentation
and quantitative reproducibility.

It was out of this gap that the present project was formulated. In
essence, the problem that was identified was the requirement of a
computational framework that would be objective, reproducible, and
efficient enough to quantify nanoparticle accumulation in
gastrointestinal tissue. In other words, the question that was asked
was: how can nanoparticle localization in cells be measured
efficiently, ensuring reproducibility, transparency, and biological
accuracy?
